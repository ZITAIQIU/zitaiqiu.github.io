Initial Ideas

According to a study by the Department of Environment and Energy (2019), transportation accounts for 20% of Australia’s total greenhouse gas emissions. We should start to consider how much carbon dioxide is generated when we travel to different places. Inspired by the above research and existing web applications, we decided to create an application that combines certain functions of a navigation application and CO2 footprint calculator.

DEVELOPMENT DETAIL

Inspiration

  • The carbon dioxide calculator is inspired by the carbon footprint calculator on the website. In this calculator, users can calculate carbon footprints from different sources, such as household energy consumption, air travel and other transportation. However, the website is very complicated to use. For example, if the user selects a vehicle, he needs to enter variables such as the specific make and model of the vehicle, gasoline type and driving distance to calculate the amount of carbon dioxide emitted during driving.

  • The inspiration for creating a navigation application came from Google Maps. It is practical and is commonly used by many people every day. It also allows users to find out the distance between their starting location and destination, as well as the time required for different transportation methods to calculate their carbon footprint. The navigation system included in the app will save users from having to enter the distance and time they travel, which makes our calculator more user-friendly.

Research

  • Many studies have shown that competition and rewards will motivate people to learn and adopt different behaviors (Berlyne, 1949; Neubert, 2019). The purpose of our project i s to encourage people to choose low-carbon travel. Therefore, by implementing a reward system and a third-party login system (Facebook, Twit, etc.), users can compete with friends in the friend list. Through this interaction, users may choose carbon-free transportation more frequently and pay more attention to reducing future carbon dioxide emissions.

  • Another study shows that interest is one of the keys to stimulating human learning and adoption behavior (Flanagan, 2017). Therefore, we implemented a scenic gallery along the route suggested by the app to increase users’ interest in walking/cycling.

UI DESIGN APPROACH

Brisbane CO2 Tracker's is minimalist design. The Brisbane CO2 tracker is a minimalist design. Due to our audience users are generally young adults. Moran (2016) conducts a study and find that young adults/Millennials (aged 18 – 25) appreciate flat design more than their parents do and like interactivity only when it serves a purpose, and they like contents that are easy to scan. Therefore, to meet the preference of our target audience, we adopt the minimalist design approach. Our main user interface is easy to follow. The dialogs for entering start location, destination, and transport method are apparent. All other functionalities of the applications are available in the navigation bar. Users can easily find each functionality through the navigation bar. The details of the website will be showed in later sections of this report.

LINKING API

The project used the Queensland Government's photo database. The database contains more than 20,000 records. Our website provides historical pictures of the place based on the destination entered by the user. Therefore, I need to search the entire database to match the target. This is also one of the difficulties I encountered. My original plan was to filter the website before loading the database, but I failed. My alternative solution is to read all databases and then filter them. The disadvantage is that the time to load images has increased, so I added a loading interface.
